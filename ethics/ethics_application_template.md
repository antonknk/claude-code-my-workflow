### A3. Abstract

> This is the pre-analysis plan for a doorstep experiment testing the effects of deliberative interactions on diffuse and specific supports. In cooperation with a political party, the experiment randomises whether doorstep interactions are deliberative or non-deliberative. In the non-deliberative condition, canvassers listen to voters' concerns and use active listening strategies after which they pitch the party's policy position on a given issue. In the deliberative condition, they additionally discuss this issue with the voter, put forward their own arguments for the party's position and respond to counterarguments. At the end of the conversation, voters are handed a physical mini-questionnaire measuring propensity to vote for the party, evaluation of party responsiveness, coalition preference, and liking of the interaction. I cross-randomise whether outcomes are measured anonymously and via a small ballot box or through the canvasser asking face-to-face to gauge social desirability bias. Deliberative interactions are expected to increase PTV, evaluations of party responsiveness, and liking of the interaction. They are also expected to improve the accuracy of canvassers' PTV guesses. Mode is expected to shift levels of the DV but not the treatment effect.

### A4. Data Collection

For this experiment, I collect data about participants in three ways, all of which are embedded in the canvassing campaign of the partner organisation (PO).

1) Canvassers are asked to report basic socio-demographics (guessed age and gender) as well as content and tone of the conversations they have with voters through standardised survey instruments.
2) Voters are asked to share their stance on three standard political attitudes (see questionnaire) with canvassers at the doorstep. Canvassers record the answers via a qualtrics survey.
3) A random subset of voters are asked to respond to the questions in 2 anonymously. They are handed a four-question mini-questionnaire (the three political items plus a question asking them how they liked the conversation). They fill out the questionnaire, fold it, and put it into an urn that canvassers carry with them.

In addition to that, I run a baseline and post-campaign survey among the canvassers, collecting socio-demographics, political attitudes, and their feedback about the campaign.

## Section G: Detailed Study Information

### G1. Participant Information

> There are two kinds of participants in the study, canvassers and residents (I also refer to residents as voters/interlocutors). Canvassers are recruited via the party. They are members who want to participate in the party's canvassing campaign. They are not being paid and their participation in both the study and the campaign itself is voluntary. The party will define a non-random group of precincts in the electoral district to canvas. Canvassers are then sent to parcels/blocks in these precincts and canvass the voters residing there. Those voters who are at home, open the door to canvassers, and engage in a conversation with them, will become participants (i.e. data from these conversations will be recorded.)


### G2. Gatekeepers

> The party is the crucial gatekeeper in this project. As a democratic membership organisation, it relies heavily on its members' support but has no direct or material power over them. Members don't have to fear consequences for not participating in the campaign. As a matter of fact, only a small minority of very active members usually participate in the campaign. I have conducted two field experiments with this organisation in the past and this project is facilitated by members' positive evaluation of previous projects that left them eager to continue the cooperation.
> In the second stage, canvassers are gatekeepers between me, the party and the voters. Given their obvious self-interest to be perceived as a trustworthy and competent political party, canvassers can be expected to behave professionally and non-obstrusively. Over the last projects, activists of partner organisation contacted arou nd 10.000 individuals and no complaints about canvasser behaviour were raised to the party (or me) over the whole process.

### G5. Informed Consent Process

> **Canvassers**: Canvassers are informed about the purpose and subject of the research when they complete the baseline survey and each time they enter the tool used to record doorstep interactions, and are briefed on the study at training events held before the campaign. Consent to take part is obtained in each survey they complete (baseline, canvassing session, post-campaign).

> **Voters**: I do not seek prior informed consent from voters before the doorstep conversation, and voters are not told that a study is taking place. This is a deliberate departure from full informed consent, and I set out below why I regard it as proportionate and inevitable. In essence, the absence of participant risk, the anonymity of respondents, implicit consent, and the absence of deception justify this approach. The reputational risk for the PO and the risk of ceiling bias necessitate it.

> First, as outlined in more detail in G6, all data is recorded in an anonymised fashion that makes re-attribution virtually impossible at any stage of the research project. The only potential hazard would be a canvasser deliberately marking questionnaires and forcing the urn to learn what a specific voter wrote. This is remote: it requires destructive, detectable tampering and correlating deposit order, for little gain. Even then, the exposure is limited to a person's answers on a small number of political questions to a canvasser they have just spoken with. I therefore consider the risk to participants very low.

> Second, the political attitude variables are measured with implicit consent. In the anonymous condition, respondents literally fill out a questionnaire, in the open-ask condition, they respond verbally to the canvasser. It seems reasonable that respondents who don't want to share this information would decline to answer. While we don't obtain implicit consent for recording socio-demographics (age group and gender), topics mentioned, and the tone of the conversation, this data is coarse and insensitive.

> Third, no deception is involved. Canvassers introduce themselves truthfully as members or supporters of Bündnis 90/Die Grünen, the visit is a genuine campaign conversation, and the questions posed at its close are of exactly the kind a party routinely asks at the doorstep. Nothing about the canvasser's identity, the nature of the visit, or the questions is misrepresented. What we withhold is that the interaction additionally serves a research study. We also don't explicitly inform voters that any data is recorded. This is non-disclosure, not the creation of a false belief.

> Seeking prior consent for the conversation itself is not feasible. Announcing the study in advance would restrict the sample to voters willing to take part in research — in practice a self-selected group of party supporters — which would undermine the experimental design and the inferences it supports. It would also wrongly imply that the party engages voters to serve a research project rather than out of genuine interest in the conversation. Parties and canvassers inevitably form impressions of voters and of public opinion from doorstep conversations, and act on them; this study does not introduce that inferential step but replaces ad hoc, memory-based, and potentially biased recollection with systematic and methodologically defensible measurement.
> Because respondents are anonymous or pseudonymous and are not re-contactable, individual debriefing and withdrawal are not practicable; the minimal, coarse, and non-identifying nature of the recorded data is the safeguard I rely on in their place.


### G6. Confidentiality and Data Management

**i) In your collection of data**
Two streams of data are collected, neither of which contains direct identifiers of voters.
The first is canvasser-entered session data, recorded in Qualtrics on the canvasser's device: brief impressions of the conversation (its tone and the topics the voter appeared to raise) and coarse socio-demographic categories (age band and gender). These fields are deliberately restricted to broad, non-identifying categories. There is no individual-level geolocation. Responses are recorded via the LSE-hosted Qualtrics environment and are not retained on the canvasser's device.
The second is the voter's own answers to the outcome questions. In the anonymous condition the voter completes a short paper questionnaire and deposits it, unseen by the canvasser, into a sealed urn. The questionnaire carries no human-readable personal identifier; it is linked to the corresponding session record only through a two-code system — an individual QR code printed on the body of the form and a separate, detachable numeric stub. The canvasser detaches and enters the stub number in the Qualtrics-app before the voter answers, so the canvasser never sees the completed questionnaire and cannot observe how a given voter responded. This makes the linkage pseudonymous: it connects a questionnaire to a session record, not to an identifiable person. In the open condition the canvasser necessarily hears the answers and enters them directly, but records them without any direct identifier for the voter; there is therefore no anonymity from the canvasser in this arm, only from third parties and from all study outputs. No item of collected data, alone or in combination, defines an identifiable individual or a targetable subgroup.
At the end of a canvassing session, canvassers list the addresses (street and number) they visited during the session. However, this data is only recorded if the canvassers knocked on at least 7 doors. That way, the campaign is able to track progress and avoid visiting addresses twice. Because the data is recorded at the session (rather than the conversation) level and because almost all houses in the electoral district under study are multi-apartment buildings, this is coarse enough not to threaten anonymity.

**ii) In your study outputs (dissertation, thesis, publications, presentations)**
All outputs report data only in aggregate or de-identified form. Socio-demographic categories are reported only as group-level statistics. Identification of subjects is neither possible nor intended.

**iii) Where will data be stored?**
Session data are held in LSE's institutionally licensed Qualtrics environment. Scanned images of the paper questionnaires and the merged analysis dataset are stored on LSE OneDrive. Physical paper questionnaires are held securely in the party office until they have been digitised and the digitisation verified, after which they are destroyed. All storage and processing takes place within the UK or the EU.

**iv) Do you plan to use Generative AI tools to analyse or otherwise interact with the information about or provided by participants?**
Yes, for one narrowly defined purpose: digitising the paper questionnaires. Canvassers photograph completed questionnaires and the images are processed with the Anthropic API to read the marked responses and resolve ambiguous marks into structured data. The images contain the ticked responses and the pseudonymous linkage codes (QR/stub) only; they carry no name, address, or other direct identifier. Local optical mark recognition is used as the first pass, with the AI applied only where marks are ambiguous, minimising what is transmitted.


### G7. Participant Risk Assessment

> Interactions between canvassers and voters at the doorstep, while common, pose everyday risks like passing on infectious diseases. While a residual risks remains, canvassers are instructed not to engage in door-to-door campaigning if they feel unwell. The intervention - manipulating the content of the conversations - should not pose any risk to participants.
> Another risk is that residents threaten to or harm canvassers. Canvassers are trained to never engage in confrontational interactions with residents and to always retreat if they feel unsafe. Canvassers are conducting the conversations in pairs.
> Canvassing is generally regarded as a safer form of outreach than e.g. distributing leaflets on high streets, where attackers can remain anonymous. However, there have been cases of verbal harassment and insulting in doorstep canvassing campaigns. In order to mitigate the psychological consequences of such experiences, canvassers thus meet for a debriefing after each canvassing session and check in on each other.

### G9. Study Benefits

> This research aims to generate knowledge that could benefit democratic participation and political communication more broadly by examining whether reciprocal, deliberative political conversations produce more positive interactions than non-deliberative ones. The findings could inform best practices for political outreach across organisations and campaigns, potentially leading to more effective and respectful campaign strategies. If reciprocal approaches prove more successful, this could encourage political organisations to adopt conversation methods that prioritise mutual exchange over one-way messaging, ultimately improving the quality of political discourse.
> From an academic perspective, the study will contribute to scholarly understanding of political behaviour, campaign effectiveness, and representation. This knowledge may inform future research on democratic participation and political mobilisation strategies, advancing our understanding of how reciprocity affects responsiveness of canvassers and effectiveness of campaigns. Finally, there are benefits to participants. Those contacted may benefit from opportunities to discuss political issues in a structured, respectful environment where their views are actively heard and valued. The deliberative conversation approach may provide participants with a more positive experience of political engagement than traditional campaign interactions, potentially encouraging greater civic participation. By fostering genuine dialogue rather than one-sided messaging, this research could contribute to more meaningful democratic engagement at the grassroots level.
